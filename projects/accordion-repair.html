Accordion Repair

One day, out of nowhere, I decided to learn how to play the accordion (believe it or not, this statement will be funnier at the end of this page). I’d seen on YouTube that it was possible to get an accordion for cheap—lots of people had gotten one when they were popular 50 or so years ago, and now their children have nothing to do with them. After a few days of checking Facebook Marketplace, though, I realized that because it’s common knowledge that accordions sell for less than they’re worth, sellers were much wiser to the value, and there were hardly any good deals. So when I saw one for $300 (expensive, but a decent price for a working accordion), I seized the opportunity.

When buying a used accordion, the internet says it’s really important to test it first to make sure there are no problems. They tend to be at least a few decades old and are extremely complex, so there’s a high chance of issues and the purchase might not be worth it. I was naive and impatient by the time I met the seller. I did test it, and I heard some horse‑esque wheezing from some of the keys—but I caved and took the deal anyway.

I then proceeded to educate myself on one of the most useless skills one could ever learn: accordion repair. It turned out to be simple conceptually, but it requires a lot of manual labor.

In brief, accordions work similarly to a harmonica: there are blocks filled with metallic reeds that produce sound when air passes through them. These “reed plates" are double‑sided, with a simple valve flap on the back of each reed, so that sound is produced both when the bellows are stretched and when they’re compressed (the reed that plays when you squeeze is actually a different one from the one that plays when you pull, even though it’s the same note).

After opening up the accordion, the problem wasn’t the reeds themselves (which would have meant buying new ones—or in other words, buying a new accordion). Instead, the leather valves that prevent air from leaking the wrong way had curled away from the metal, letting air through and causing the awful gasping whine. So after cleaning out the internals with a copious amount of cotton swabs, I started the painstaking process of replacing the valves.

All in all there are 3 reed compartments, each with 21 reed plates on each side. Each reed plate has 2 reeds and thus 2 valves. That’s 252 valves on the right side of the accordion. On the left (bass) side there’s another 12×2×2×2 = 96 valves. In total I needed to check 348 valves. Great bouncing icebergs—that’s a lot!

Plastic valves (and tiny holes)

According to the internet, we’ve invented plastic since these things were made, and now these valves are typically made from plastic sheets. I bought plastic valves and started replacing every problematic one.

Remember: there are two reeds per reed plate, and there’s a reed plate on each side of the reed block, so every reed plate has a reed and valve on the inside too. The only way to access that side is to remove the plate from the block (which can mess with the accordion’s tuning), or there’s a tiny hole on the bottom of the block where you can barely reach the valve. In fear of breaking the accordion, I ended up painstakingly pulling out and replacing valves with tweezers through these tiny holes. The valves had to be placed exactly right or air would leak, and I was essentially working blind—the hole was small and narrow enough that lighting it and viewing it at the same time was pretty much impossible.

To avoid completely disassembling and reassembling the accordion every time I wanted to test a valve, I took inspiration from a YouTube video and 3D‑printed a custom tester. It sounds fancy, but it’s essentially just a board that fits into the bellows with a hole on top, so you can test a single note.

After replacing all the problematic valves, I reassembled it and… it sounded terrible. For the larger valves (which was all of the ones I’d replaced, since the larger ones tended to curl), plastic doesn’t do a good job—the valve can flap around and cause a secondary “pbbbbth” alongside the intended tone. Cool.

After more research, I saw that it’s also common to glue “springs” to the back of the valves—thin strips of metal that resist any non‑elastic deformation. I bought springs online, removed the plastic valves, put the original leather valves back, and glued the springs on. That didn’t work either; the springs weren’t stiff enough to hold the leather to the reed plate. As a last attempt, I tried making my own valve springs by 3D‑printing a single line of filament onto the build plate and cutting it to size. To my dismay, it worked perfectly. So I didn’t need to buy replacement parts, and I didn’t actually have to replace the valves themselves—but the accordion works.

So, after all of that very difficult work, I have my accordion, ready for practice. Which I have practiced with for a total of four hours over the past year :P.
