Sharknado Wiimote Booth Game

When the next year rolled around, I decided I wanted to really test my limits with the next booth game. Our club had chosen Sharknado as the booth’s theme and, to match this blockbuster franchise, I wanted something cinematic and engaging. So I went in with the rule that I would add every feature that popped into my head, no matter the complexity. As you’ll see, that turned out to be a bit more than I could handle.

The main idea was a multiplayer game controlled with Wii remotes, where players fight off sharks flying toward them. One player uses a lightgun to point at the screen and shoot, while the other wields a “chainsaw” they can swing around. Using intuitive controls avoids a big learning curve and increases throughput at the booth, since two people can play at once. I also had the genius idea of setting the game on CMU’s campus, totally out of love for the school (and definitely not to pander to the judges, wink).

The big question: how do I get CMU’s campus into the game? Early on I naively tried to model campus in 3D. That might be fine for someone with Blender experience; I had almost none with non‑parametric modeling. I tried grabbing the model from Google Maps, but it was too low‑quality—especially the textures.

Then I got the genius idea: if the game is based on a mp4ie with cheap special effects, why not just use real footage of CMU as the background? As long as the footage was motion tracked accurately, I could put anything I wanted into it. So during spring break I went out and filmed a first‑person walk from the Fence to Hamerschlag Hall.

Overall, the video processing/editing took about a month, most of it spent motion tracking the camera. I naively recorded at 1080p/30fps because that’s what the projector could display—but I ended up desperately needing that extra information to improve feature tracking, especially during quick turns when the image blurred. I tried several apps and Blender extensions, but in the end I had to track every single point manually.

Once the motion tracking was done, I had to actually convey that there are sharknados everywhere. Putting the tornadoes right next to the camera would be too chaotic, so I placed them behind CMU’s buildings. That meant I had to mask out the buildings from the sky. Normally you’d chroma‑key the sky color, but it had rained earlier that day and the roofs were reflecting the sky—which is exactly the edge I needed to separate. So I spent another two weeks manually rotoscoping all of the roofs. At this point it was easy to forget I was making a video game and not a full‑blown mp4ie.

Despite the hurdles, the rest of the video work went smoothly. I quickly animated tornadoes of various widths and used Blender physics to procedurally animate sharks in orbit. After some careful placement, the video was complete.

By the time I got to actual gameplay, I only had a few days left. I frantically spent every spare moment wiring everything up. Thankfully, someone had already made an API for Wii remote → Unity communication, so I could extract motion controls for the chainsaw.

I wanted it to be very clear that the sharks were being sliced apart, so I wrote code to procedurally bisect the model along the direction of the Wiimote swing, and it worked perfectly. For the lightgun, I used a DolphinBar to convert the Wiimote pointer into mouse input, and created shattered shark models that were swapped in at the moment of impact so the sharks appear to explode into pieces.

To make the game feel more arcade‑like and immersive, I also built gun/chainsaw‑shaped holsters for the Wiimotes. The chainsaw was especially awkward: lots of uniquely-shaped parts with high breakage potential.

Near the end I realized there wasn’t a good narrative reason for the protagonist to be running to Hamerschlag Hall. Wouldn’t it be easier to just hide in the nearest building? So I decided there must be a sharknado‑destroying weapon inside. Conveniently, CMU Radio Club has its space at the top of Hamerschlag, and after a few emails they agreed to let me film there.

I decided it would be hilarious if the sharknado‑ending weapon were some sort of satellite laser that ends up destroying the entire city, so I made a simple Blender animation for that too.
