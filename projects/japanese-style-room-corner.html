Japanese-style Room

When I started my last summer as a college student, I realized this could be the last time I’d spend a significant amount of time living in my parents’ house. I wanted to help make their soon-to-be empty nest a little more comfortable. This page covers the renovation I did for my dad (my mom’s renovation is under The Mega-Cabinet + Desk).

Background

My dad’s father is an immigrant from Taiwan and grew up there when the country was occupied by Japan. So even though we’ve lived mostly as a typical American family, we’ve kept a few traditions from my grandfather, and Japanese culture has a small place in our hearts. My dad also grew up in a house that was a mishmash of different cultural styles, with a tokonoma and a shoji-style window tucked into one side of the living room as the most distinct feature.

Over the years he’d told me he planned to convert the office in our house into a Japanese-style room. He’d already put in a hanging light and a low table (chabudai), but the office had become increasingly cluttered with old documents and was a tough beast to approach—and he didn’t have the energy anymore to redesign an entire room. I offered to coordinate with him to bring the room to life (though “bulldozed” might be the better word; he was somewhat disapproving of me sinking a ton of time into this on his behalf).

Planning

We spent dozens of hours on planning and design—it was meant to be for him, so I didn’t want to impose too much of my own ideas. We decided on: tatami in the large center area of the floor; because tatami is a few inches thick and we couldn’t lower the OSB subflooring, we’d raise the entire area around it to match and give it custom wooden flooring; on the far side of the room we’d add a wall to create an alcove for the tokonoma, like in the house he grew up in; the space on the other side of that wall would become a closet with new shelving to store the documents that had cluttered the room; and we’d add real wood trim around the room and large wooden beams around the tokonoma (they’re traditionally made with a large central trunk or pole).

Wood and custom cuts

One of the parts we were most afraid of was shaping and cutting the wood beams and trim—everything needed to be custom-cut, which we thought would require an actual woodshop. When we visited our local supplier, we not only found beautiful sapele but they could do the custom cuts. In two hours we had two large beams and about ten small 1″×0.25″ trim pieces all cut to dimension (wish I could’ve shaken the hand of whoever cut those).

Build

I was doing an internship at the same time, so most of my summer was: go to work, come home, then get to work on the room. That made it possible to finish before I went back to school in a month or two, but it also meant dragging my dad back into the room to help, sometimes into the later hours of the night.
