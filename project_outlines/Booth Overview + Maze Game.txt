Every year, our school hosts a celebration known as spring carnival. For this event, many clubs will create “booths”, which are essentially temporary wooden structures themed around some pop culture subject that carnival-goers can walk through. So, for my sophomore year, I decided to join my club’s booth effort as a booth chair, responsible for the construction of our booth in that year’s spring carnival. This year, our club wanted to do a parody on monopoly as the theme of our booth. 


Despite there being 6 different booth chairs for the club as well as sufficient funding for materials and the like, the build process was always a race against time. From December to April, we had bi-weekly builds where we needed to essentially prepare the booth for assembly during “build week” right before carnival. To begin, we needed to create plans for structure (CAD models), wiring, and game design which had to be verified by the Spring Carnival Committee (SCC) in multiple stages throughout the year. Then we needed to prep all of the wall studs for assembly, recutting/reassembling the walls from previous years to fit the current design. There was taking inventory of all of the inner and outer wall ply, cutting it to size, and then painting it with primer and the booth’s decorations. We also had to rebuild the floor and ceiling, re-cut leveling blocks, buy new osb+plywood for the floor, ceiling, and walls, as well as waterproofing since it often rains during carnival time.

When build week arrived, all of us build chairs then spent the next week working on and directing the construction of the booth itself, assembling the leveling blocks, floor, floor osb, wall studs, inner/outer wall ply, top runners, ceiling studs, inner ceiling ply, outer roof ply, waterproofing, electrical, painting, etc, etc. What was particularly challenging was not only the physical labor involved, but also the leadership work, as all of the booth chairs were responsible for directing the other members of the club to do their work efficiently. The booth also had to be held to a strict building code, as members of SCC would make periodic checks to ensure that everything was up to safety regulations (which are extremely strict).

Additionally, each booth chair was given a different role based on the tasking that needed to get done. To my delight, I was given the role of game chair. I wanted to make a simple marble maze game for the booth, however between classes and build sessions I kept putting off its construction. So, after the last day of build week, I went home and spent the entire night into the morning building the marble maze. I was able to hastily lazercut the maze walls, however I had to manually cut out the rest (including the tunnel between the end and the start) with a box cutter. It ended up turning out decent, though by the end of carnival it had been smashed to bits by the kids that played with it.
