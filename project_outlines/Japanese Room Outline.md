When I started my last summer as a college student, I realized that this could be the last time I would stay a significant amount of time living in my parent’s house. Thus, I thought it would be a good time to help make my parent’s soon-to-be empty nest be a little more comfortable. This will cover the renovation I did for my dad (If you want to see what I did for my mom, that renovation for is under the project name “The Mega-Cabinet/Desk”). 

As some background, my dad’s father is an immigrant from Taiwan, who grew up there during the time when the country was occupied by Japan. Thus, despite us living mostly as a typical American family, we’ve kept a few traditions from my grandfather, which has earned Japanese culture a small place in our hearts. My dad also grew up with a house that was a mishmash of different cultural styles, with the most distinct feature being a tokonoma and a shoji-style window tucked into one side of the living room: 

(Picture here)

Thus, on many occasions, he had told me that he planned on converting the office in our house into a Japanese-style room. He’d even put in a hanging light, as well as a low table (“chubadai”), however due to the office becoming increasingly cluttered with old documents, it was a tough beast to approach, especially since he didn’t have the energy anymore to just up and redesign an entire room. Thus, I offered to coordinate with him in order to bring this room to life (though “bulldozed” might be the better term, since he was somewhat disapproving of the prospect of me sinking a ton of time into this project on his behalf).

At the beginning, we spent dozens of hours on planning and design – it was meant to be for him, after all, so I didn’t want to impose too much of my own ideas onto the final product. Eventually, we decided on a list of what he wanted. We’d put tatami in the large center area of the floor, and since the tatami is a few inches thick and we can’t lower the OSB layer of the subflooring, we’d have to raise the entire area around it in order to match the thickness (and also give it custom wooden flooring). On the far side of the room, we would add a wall to create an alcove for the tokonoma, just like in the house he grew up in. Since that created an unused space on the other side, we decided to make the other side into a closet space with new shelving that could compactly store the documents that currently cluttered the room. Additionally, we decided to add real wood trim around the room, as well as large wooden beams around the tokonoma, since they traditionally seem to be made using a large central trunk or pole. 

One of the parts we were most afraid of was shaping and cutting the wood beams and trim – they needed to be custom-cut, which would require an actual woodshop in order to pull off. However, when we visited our local supplier, not only did we find some beautiful sapele, but they also were able to do custom cuts. In two hours flat, we had two pristine large beams as well as 10 or so small 1”x0.25” trim beams all perfectly to dimension (wish I could’ve shook the hand of whoever cut those, honestly). 

After many, many orders and trips to the store, we got set to work on building. I was doing an internship at the same time, so most of my summer ended up being going to work, coming home and then immediately getting to work on the room. This ensured that the project would be complete before I went back for school in the next month or two, but it also meant dragging my poor dad back into the room to help me, sometimes into the later hours of the night. Explaining the entire build process and all of its twists and turns would take a looong time so I’ll just show you some pictures and caption them with fun facts (which hopefully you find interesting).
